01

Governance Definition & Levels

TOGAF definition

Architecture Governance is the practice and orientation by which enterprise architectures and other architectures are managed and controlled at an enterprise-wide level. It ensures architecture decisions are made consistently, transparently, and in alignment with business strategy.

TOGAF recognises that governance operates at multiple levels — from corporate governance at the top, through IT governance in the middle, to architecture governance at the detailed level. Each level has different concerns, participants, and decision rights.

Corporate
Corporate Governance
Board-level governance of the entire organisation. Concerned with strategic direction, risk, compliance, and accountability to shareholders and regulators. Sets the context for all lower governance levels.
Technology
Technology / IT Governance
Governance of IT investment, delivery, and operations. Concerned with IT budget, project delivery, IT risk, and ensuring IT serves business needs. Includes frameworks like COBIT and ISO 38500.
Architecture
Architecture Governance
Governance of the enterprise architecture — ensuring architectures are consistent, principles-based, and aligned to strategy. The Architecture Board is the primary governance body at this level.
Governance principles in TOGAF

TOGAF governance is: Disciplined (follows defined processes), Accountable (clear responsibility for decisions), Transparent (decisions are visible and auditable), Independent (free from conflicts of interest), and Effective (achieves the intended outcome without excessive overhead).

⭐ Exam tip

TOGAF recognises three governance levels: Corporate → Technology/IT → Architecture. Architecture governance sits within the broader IT governance framework, which itself sits within corporate governance. Each level has different scope and participants.

02

Architecture Board — Composition & Responsibilities

The Architecture Board

The Architecture Board is the cross-organisational body responsible for architecture governance. It has the authority to review, approve, and mandate changes to architecture — and to grant or refuse dispensations for non-conforming implementations.

Composition

Typically includes: CIO or IT Director, Chief Architect, senior business representatives from key business units, domain architecture leads (Business, Data, Application, Technology Architects), and external independent members where needed. Composition varies by organisation size.

Responsibilities

Review and approve all architecture deliverables. Grant dispensations for non-conforming implementations. Resolve architecture conflicts between projects. Maintain architecture standards and principles. Report to executive leadership on architecture status and risks.

Decision rights

The Board has formal authority to: approve or reject architecture deliverables, grant or refuse dispensations, require changes to implementations, and escalate unresolved issues to executive leadership. Its authority is delegated from the executive sponsor.

Operating model

The Board typically meets regularly (monthly or quarterly) for routine reviews, and convenes ad hoc for urgent compliance issues or major change requests. Decisions are recorded in the Governance Log.

⭐ Exam tip

The Architecture Board's three most important functions: reviewing and approving architectures, granting dispensations, and maintaining the Governance Log. It provides formal governance authority — without it, architecture compliance is only advisory.

03

Architecture Contracts — Purpose & Content

Revisiting Architecture Contracts

Architecture Contracts formalise the relationship between the architecture team and implementation projects. They define what will be built, what standards apply, and what governance oversight will occur. They give the Architecture Board the formal authority to require conformance.

Purpose

To create a formal, binding agreement between development partners and sponsors on the deliverables, quality, and fitness-for-purpose of the architecture being implemented. Without contracts, governance is advisory only.

When produced

Drafted in Phase F (Migration Planning) as part of delivery preparation. Finalised and signed at the start of Phase G (Implementation Governance) before implementation work begins.

Types

Design Contracts govern the architecture and design work itself. Business and IT Interface Contracts govern what IT will deliver to the business and at what quality. Both types may coexist for a single implementation project.

Key contents

Architecture to be implemented, conformance requirements, applicable principles and standards, agreed quality levels, exceptions already granted, procedures for requesting dispensations, and governance procedures for the project.

⭐ Exam tip

Architecture Contracts are drafted in Phase F and signed in Phase G. Two types: Design Contracts (architecture design work) and Business and IT Interface Contracts (IT delivery to business). Without them, the Board has no formal authority to enforce conformance.

04

Compliance vs Conformance (revisited)

Building on what was introduced in Phase G (Day 9), this section looks at the compliance vs conformance distinction in the broader governance context and how it applies to architecture governance frameworks.

TermDefinitionAssessed againstWho decides
ConformanceDoes the implementation match the approved architecture?Architecture Definition Document and Architecture ContractsArchitecture Board
ComplianceDoes the implementation meet all applicable governance requirements — principles, standards, regulations?Full architecture governance framework including regulatory requirementsArchitecture Board + Compliance functions
DispensationFormal permission to deviate from a specific conformance requirementSpecific conformance requirement in the Architecture ContractArchitecture Board (formal approval required)
WaiverExemption from a compliance requirement — typically granted for regulatory reasons or documented risk acceptanceSpecific compliance requirementArchitecture Board + Legal/Compliance
Why the distinction matters in governance

Conformance is the narrower question: does this match the architecture? Compliance is the broader question: does this meet all the rules? A project can be fully conformant to its Architecture Contract but still non-compliant — for example, if the architecture itself failed to incorporate a regulatory requirement. Governance must address both dimensions.

⭐ Exam tip

Conformance = matches the architecture. Compliance = meets all governance requirements. A dispensation = permission to deviate from a conformance requirement. Both are formal processes requiring Architecture Board involvement and Governance Log recording.

05

IT Governance vs Architecture Governance

A critical distinction

IT Governance and Architecture Governance are related but distinct. Understanding the difference — and how they complement each other — is important for the exam and for practice.

DimensionIT GovernanceArchitecture Governance
Primary concernIT investment, delivery, and operationsArchitecture coherence, principles, and standards
Key frameworkCOBIT, ISO 38500TOGAF Architecture Governance
Governing bodyIT Steering Committee, CIOArchitecture Board, Chief Architect
Key outputsIT investment decisions, project approvals, SLA managementArchitecture approvals, dispensations, compliance assessments
Primary recordIT project portfolio, risk registerArchitecture Repository, Governance Log
ScopeAll IT investment and operationsArchitecture of IT and business systems
How they work together

Architecture Governance operates within the broader IT Governance framework. IT Governance approves IT investment budgets; Architecture Governance ensures those investments conform to the enterprise architecture. A project might need approval from both the IT Steering Committee (budget) and the Architecture Board (architectural conformance) before it can proceed.

⭐ Exam tip

IT Governance = concerned with IT investment, delivery, operations. Architecture Governance = concerned with architecture coherence and conformance. Architecture Governance operates within IT Governance. They are complementary, not competing.
