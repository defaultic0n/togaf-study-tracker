01

Establishing an Architecture Practice

What the Capability Framework addresses

The Architecture Capability Framework covers how to establish and operate the organisational structures, processes, roles, and responsibilities needed to successfully run enterprise architecture using TOGAF. It answers: "How do you make EA happen in your organisation?"

Running the ADM requires more than just following the process. It requires the right people in the right roles, the right governance structures, the right tools and repositories, and the right level of organisational commitment. The Capability Framework provides a blueprint for establishing all of this.

Architecture Organisation

The formal team and structures through which architecture work is conducted — including the Architecture Board, Architecture Review Committees, and the architecture function within IT and business units.

Architecture Processes

The defined, repeatable processes for conducting architecture work — the ADM cycle, governance processes, review processes, and change management processes that architecture teams follow.

Architecture Skills

The competencies and skills required of individuals working in architecture roles — business analysis, technology understanding, communication, leadership, and governance expertise.

Architecture Principles

The fundamental rules and guidelines that all architecture work must adhere to — established in the Preliminary Phase and underpinning all ADM decisions.

⭐ Exam tip

The Capability Framework is about establishing the capability to do architecture — not about doing the architecture itself. It covers the organisation, processes, skills, and principles needed to run the ADM effectively. The Preliminary Phase establishes the architecture capability using the Capability Framework.

02

Architecture Maturity Models

Purpose

Architecture maturity models assess how well-developed an organisation's architecture capability is — and provide a roadmap for improvement. They help organisations understand where they are now and what they need to do to reach a higher level of capability.

TOGAF references several maturity models, including the US Department of Commerce ACMM (Architecture Capability Maturity Model). Most follow a similar 0–5 scale:

0
None
No architecture capability exists. Architecture work is ad hoc and undocumented.
1
Initial
Architecture processes exist but are informal and person-dependent. Not consistently applied.
2
Under Development
Architecture processes are being defined and documented. Partial governance in place.
3
Defined
Architecture processes are documented, standardised, and consistently applied across the organisation.
4
Managed
Architecture processes are measured, monitored, and actively managed. Quantitative metrics used.
5
Optimising
Continuous improvement is built into the architecture process. Innovation and best-practice adoption are routine.
⭐ Exam tip

Architecture maturity is assessed on a 0–5 scale: 0 (None) → 1 (Initial) → 2 (Under Development) → 3 (Defined) → 4 (Managed) → 5 (Optimising). Most organisations start at level 1 when adopting TOGAF and target level 3 as the minimum effective maturity.

03

Architecture Skills Framework

Purpose

The Architecture Skills Framework defines the competencies and skills required for different architecture roles — providing a basis for recruitment, training, and career development of architecture professionals.

TOGAF's Skills Framework organises architecture competencies into several skill categories:

Generic Skills

Communication, leadership, stakeholder management, negotiation, and presentation skills. Essential at all levels — architecture is fundamentally a people-facing discipline.

Business Skills and Methods

Business analysis, strategy, financial management, organisational change management. Architects must understand business, not just technology.

Enterprise Architecture Skills

Architecture frameworks (TOGAF), modelling methods, architecture patterns, governance, and the specific technical knowledge for each BDAT domain.

Program and Project Management

Understanding of programme management, project planning, benefits realisation, and change delivery. Architects must work alongside delivery teams effectively.

IT General Knowledge

Broad technology literacy — infrastructure, cloud, security, data, software development, integration. Not expert-level in all areas but sufficient to have informed technical conversations.

⭐ Exam tip

The Skills Framework defines competencies for architecture roles. Key point for the exam: generic and business skills are as important as technical skills for architects. TOGAF does not define architecture as a purely technical role — communication, stakeholder management, and business acumen are core competencies.

04

Key Roles: Chief Architect, Architecture Board, Sponsor

Chief Architect
Leads the architecture function. Accountable for the quality and consistency of the enterprise architecture. Chairs or reports to the Architecture Board. Sets the architectural direction and enforces architecture principles.
Architecture Board
Cross-organisational governance body. Reviews and approves architectures, grants dispensations, resolves conflicts. Provides the formal authority for architecture governance. Typically includes the CIO, Chief Architect, and senior stakeholders.
Architecture Sponsor
Senior executive who sponsors and funds the architecture function and specific architecture engagements. Issues the Request for Architecture Work. Provides organisational authority and resources.
Enterprise Architect
Conducts the architecture work — running ADM phases, producing deliverables, engaging stakeholders. May specialise in Business, Data, Application, or Technology architecture domains.
Domain Architects
Specialists within a BDAT domain — Business Architect, Data Architect, Application Architect, Technology Architect. Provide depth of expertise within their domain during ADM phases B, C, and D.
Solution Architect
Works at the project level, designing solutions that conform to the enterprise architecture. Operates within the constraints defined by Enterprise Architects and the Architecture Board.
⭐ Exam tip

The Architecture Board provides governance authority. The Architecture Sponsor provides funding and issues the Request for Architecture Work. The Chief Architect leads the architecture function. These three roles are the most frequently tested in the Capability Framework section.

05

Architecture Centre of Excellence

What an ACE is

An Architecture Centre of Excellence (ACE) — sometimes called an Enterprise Architecture Centre of Excellence — is a dedicated organisational unit that houses the architecture function, develops architecture capability, and provides architecture services to the rest of the organisation.

Architecture Practice Development

The ACE develops and maintains the architecture framework, methods, templates, and patterns used by architecture teams. It owns the Architecture Repository and ensures its currency and quality.

Architecture Services

The ACE provides architecture services to business units and project teams — architecture advice, compliance reviews, stakeholder engagement, and architecture risk assessment.

Capability Building

The ACE develops architecture skills across the organisation — training, mentoring, certification support, and embedding architecture thinking in business and IT teams.

Architecture Governance Support

The ACE supports the Architecture Board — preparing materials, tracking compliance, maintaining the Governance Log, and providing analytical support for governance decisions.

ACE placement in the organisation

The ACE is typically placed close to the CIO or CTO function to ensure it has authority and access. In some organisations it reports directly to the CEO. Placement signals organisational commitment — an ACE buried deep in IT is less effective than one with direct executive access and cross-business mandate.

⭐ Exam tip

The Architecture Centre of Excellence is the organisational home of the architecture function. It develops capability, provides services, and supports governance. Know that its placement close to executive leadership is important for effectiveness and authority.
