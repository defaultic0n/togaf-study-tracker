01

The ADM as TOGAF's Core Process

Core definition

The Architecture Development Method (ADM) is the process component at the heart of TOGAF. It provides a tested, repeatable, iterative method for developing an enterprise architecture — telling architects exactly what to do, in what order, and with what inputs and outputs at each step.

Everything else in TOGAF — the Content Framework, Enterprise Continuum, Reference Models, Capability Framework — exists to support the ADM. Understanding the ADM is the single most important thing you can do to prepare for the Foundation exam.

The ADM is structured as a cycle, not a linear process. This is deliberate: as the enterprise evolves, the architecture must evolve too. An organisation may run through the ADM many times, each iteration refining and extending the architecture.

Why it's a cycle

At the end of Phase H, the organisation re-evaluates and may restart the cycle — either at the Preliminary Phase (major transformation) or at Phase A (incremental change). The ADM is designed for continuous architecture evolution, not a one-time project.

⭐ Exam tip

Know the full sequence of ADM phases. The exam frequently presents phases out of order and asks you to identify the correct sequence. Also: the Preliminary Phase comes before Phase A — it is not the same as Phase A.

02

Preliminary Phase — Framework & Principles

Objective

The Preliminary Phase is about "getting ready" — preparing the organisation to undertake enterprise architecture work. It is where TOGAF is tailored to fit the enterprise's specific needs, and where the foundational principles and governance structures are put in place before any architecture work begins.

The Preliminary Phase is triggered when an organisation decides it needs an architecture capability or needs to update its existing capability. It happens before the first ADM cycle properly begins — you cannot jump straight to Phase A without completing the Preliminary Phase.

Think of it as laying the foundations of a building. You would not start construction without checking the ground, deciding how deep to dig, and preparing your tools. The Preliminary Phase does exactly this for architecture work.

Architecture Capability

The combination of skills, roles, processes, tools, and governance that allows an organisation to effectively perform architecture work. The Preliminary Phase creates or updates this capability.

Architecture Framework

The selected approach and structure for conducting architecture work. The organisation chooses and tailors TOGAF (and possibly integrates other frameworks) during the Preliminary Phase.

Architecture Governance Framework

The processes and structures that govern architecture decisions and compliance. Established during the Preliminary Phase — covers the Architecture Board, review processes, and contracts.

Enterprise Architecture Principles

The guiding rules and guidelines that will govern all architecture work going forward. Defined during the Preliminary Phase and applied in every subsequent phase.

The key outputs of the Preliminary Phase provide the foundation for everything that follows. They answer the questions: who does architecture here, how is it done, what tools are used, and what principles guide decisions?

⭐ Exam tip

The Preliminary Phase is not part of the lettered cycle (A–H). It comes before Phase A. Questions that ask "what comes before Phase A?" or "when are principles defined?" both point to the Preliminary Phase.

03

Architecture Principles — Definition & Examples

Architecture Principles are one of the most important outputs of the Preliminary Phase. They shape every architecture decision made throughout the ADM cycle — and they are frequently tested in the Foundation exam.

Definition

Architecture Principles are general rules and guidelines, intended to be enduring and seldom amended, that inform and support the way in which an organisation sets about fulfilling its mission.

Principles are not operational rules (which change frequently) or policies (which are more specific). They are high-level, durable statements that represent what the organisation values and believes about how IT and business should interact.

Every architecture principle has exactly four components — and the exam tests this structure:

Structure of an Architecture Principle
NameA short, memorable identifier for the principle. Should convey the essence in a few words. Example: "Data is an Asset"
StatementA concise, unambiguous statement of the rule. Should be testable — you should be able to say whether a decision follows this principle or not.
RationaleThe business reason why this principle exists. Explains the value of following it and the risk of ignoring it. Links back to business strategy.
ImplicationsWhat following this principle means in practice — the requirements and costs on the business and IT. This is often the longest part.

TOGAF groups principles into four categories that mirror the architecture domains:

Example: "Data is an Asset"
NameData is an Asset
StatementData is an asset that has value to the enterprise and is managed accordingly.
RationaleData is a valuable resource that, like other assets, must be safeguarded and properly managed to ensure it remains useful and accessible to those who need it.
ImplicationsRequires investment in data management. Means data ownership must be assigned. Implies costs for data governance, quality management, and access controls.

Other common examples include: Technology Independence (avoid dependency on specific vendors), Single Source of Truth (each data element has one authoritative source), Reuse Before Buy Before Build, Security by Design, and Common Vocabulary (all information systems use the same definitions).

⭐ Exam tip

Know the four components of a principle: Name, Statement, Rationale, Implications. If a question asks "which component explains why a principle exists?" the answer is Rationale. If it asks "which component describes what it costs to follow the principle?" the answer is Implications.

04

Tailoring TOGAF to the Enterprise

TOGAF is explicitly designed to be tailored. No organisation should apply it "out of the box" — doing so is actually considered incorrect use of the framework. Tailoring happens primarily in the Preliminary Phase.

Why tailoring is necessary

Every organisation has different maturity levels, existing frameworks, cultural norms, regulatory requirements, and strategic priorities. TOGAF must be adapted to work within these constraints — not replace them.

Tailoring covers a wide range of decisions. The organisation must choose which parts of TOGAF to use, how to integrate TOGAF with existing frameworks (like ITIL, COBIT, or Zachman), and how to adapt the ADM to fit its own project governance.

🔧
Selecting which TOGAF components to use

Not every organisation needs all six TOGAF components. A small organisation may focus on ADM and Content Framework only, deferring the Capability Framework until maturity grows.

🔗
Integrating with other frameworks

TOGAF is designed to coexist with ITIL (service management), COBIT (IT governance), PRINCE2 (project management), and Zachman (architecture framework). The Preliminary Phase maps these relationships.

🔄
Adapting ADM phases and iteration

Phases may be combined, shortened, or iterated differently. An Agile organisation might run Architecture Vision (Phase A) in short sprints rather than as a single long engagement.

🏛️
Establishing governance structures

The organisation defines its Architecture Board, review cycles, contract templates, and compliance criteria — all tailored to the enterprise's existing governance model.

📏
Defining architecture scope and boundaries

The enterprise scope (which parts of the organisation are covered), architecture horizon (strategic, segment, or capability level), and time horizons are all defined during tailoring.

Key TOGAF principle on tailoring

The TOGAF standard states: "TOGAF is designed to be used in conjunction with other frameworks." It is not a closed system — it expects to be integrated, combined, and adapted. An organisation that uses TOGAF alongside COBIT for governance and ITIL for operations is using it correctly.

⭐ Exam tip

Tailoring questions often present scenarios: "An organisation already uses COBIT for IT governance. How should they approach adopting TOGAF?" The answer always involves integrating them — TOGAF should be tailored to complement existing frameworks, not replace them.

05

Preliminary Phase — Key Inputs & Outputs

Every ADM phase is defined by its objectives, inputs, steps, and outputs. Knowing the key inputs and outputs for the Preliminary Phase is essential exam preparation.

Key Inputs
  • TOGAF and other architecture framework resources
  • Board strategies, business plans, IT strategy
  • Major frameworks in use (ITIL, COBIT, etc.)
  • Existing governance and legal frameworks
  • Architecture capability maturity assessments
  • Partnership and contract agreements
  • Existing organisational model for EA
Key Outputs
  • Tailored Architecture Framework (including principles)
  • Initial Architecture Repository
  • Restatement of business goals and drivers
  • Architecture Principles
  • Architecture Governance Framework
  • Architecture Capability defined
  • Request for Architecture Work (triggers Phase A)

The single most important output to remember is the Request for Architecture Work. This document formally triggers the move from the Preliminary Phase into Phase A. Without it, Phase A cannot begin — it is the gate between preparation and execution.

The steps of the Preliminary Phase

TOGAF specifies six steps: (1) Scope the enterprise organisations impacted; (2) Confirm governance and support frameworks; (3) Define and establish the Architecture Team; (4) Identify and establish Architecture Principles; (5) Tailor TOGAF and other frameworks; (6) Implement Architecture Tools.

The Architecture Repository

One of the key outputs is the initial Architecture Repository — the storage system for all architecture assets. It holds the Architecture Landscape, Reference Library, Standards Library, Solutions Portfolio, and Governance Log. It is initialised in the Preliminary Phase but used and populated throughout all subsequent phases.

⭐ Exam tip

The exam frequently asks what triggers Phase A. The answer is the Request for Architecture Work — produced in the Preliminary Phase. Also remember: Architecture Principles are an output of the Preliminary Phase, not an input to it.
