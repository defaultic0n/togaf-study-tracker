Section 1 of 5 · ADM Iteration

ADM Iteration
& Cycles

The ADM diagram looks sequential — but in practice, iteration is central to how it works. Part 3 of the TOGAF Standard defines how and when to iterate.

Why iteration exists

A single linear pass through the ADM cannot adequately handle the complexity of large-scale enterprise architecture. Iteration allows architects to refine, revisit, and progressively elaborate their work.

2 / 20

The 4 ADM Iteration Cycles

TOGAF defines four types of iteration cycle, each serving a different purpose:

Architecture Capability Iteration

Creates and evolves the architecture capability itself — establishing or adjusting the architecture approach, principles, scope, and governance.

Architecture Development Iteration

Creates architecture content by cycling through Business, IS, and Technology phases — converging on a coherent target architecture.

Transition Planning Iteration

Creates formal change roadmaps — the Architecture Roadmap and Implementation & Migration Plan.

Architecture Governance Iteration

Governs the implementation of change progressing towards the defined Target Architecture.

3 / 20

Iteration Between ADM Cycles

Multiple complete ADM cycles can run at different levels of the Architecture Landscape — each driven by its own Request for Architecture Work.

Strategic Architecture

Broad, shallow — whole enterprise view. Sets context for all lower-level work. Runs its own ADM cycle.

Segment Architecture

A business unit or functional area. Constrained by the Strategic Architecture above it.

Capability Architecture

Specific capability or solution. Most detailed. Bounded by the Segment Architecture.

★ Exam tip

These three levels — Strategic, Segment, Capability — map directly to the Architecture Landscape levels and the three Classes of Architecture Engagement covered next.

4 / 20

Baseline First vs Target First

Within an ADM cycle, two approaches can be taken:

Baseline First

Assess the current state first to identify problems and improvement opportunities. Best when the baseline is complex or poorly understood — common where business units have had high autonomy.

Target First

Elaborate the target solution in detail first, then map back to baseline to identify change. Best when a target state is already agreed at a high level and the enterprise wants to transition effectively.

★ Exam tip

The exam may ask which approach suits a given scenario. Messy, complex, highly autonomous environment = Baseline First. Clear agreed vision, known target = Target First.

Section 2 of 5 · Classes of Engagement ★ Exam Focus

Classes of
Architecture
Engagement

TOGAF Part 3 §2.3 defines three areas of architecture engagement — contexts in which an architecture function is called upon to help the enterprise.

★ Why this matters in the exam

Foundation questions frequently describe a scenario and ask which class of engagement applies, or ask you to match an engagement type to its iteration focus.

6 / 20

The Three Engagement Areas

Area 1
Identification of Required Change
Architecture as a technique to provide visibility of IT capability for strategic decision-making and alignment. Used outside any specific change initiative.
Area 2
Definition of Change
Where a need to change has been identified, architecture defines the nature and extent of that change in a structured way.
Area 3
Implementation of Change
Architecture provides design governance to change initiatives — big-picture visibility, structural constraints, and criteria for evaluating technical decisions.
7 / 20

6 Engagement Types in Detail

The three areas break down into six specific engagement types:

Supporting Business Strategy

EA provides visibility of change opportunities, tests feasibility of strategic choices. Scope: broad, shallow, baseline first.

Architectural Portfolio Management of the Landscape

Links IT portfolio performance to strategic needs. Identifies misalignment and change opportunities.

Architectural Portfolio Management of Projects

Adds architectural factors to project priority and funding decisions. Links project scope to architectural impact.

8 / 20

6 Engagement Types (continued)

Architectural Definition of Foundational Change Initiatives

Change efforts with a known objective but not yet scoped. Architecture brings structure to problem definition — baseline first, then vision.

Architectural Definition of Bounded Change Initiatives

Change efforts where the desired outcome is agreed. Architecture elaborates the target — target first, using the agreed vision as constraint.

Architectural Governance of Change Implementation

Ensures projects realise intended benefit, stay aligned with each other and with business need. Governance iteration cycle.

Architecture Partitioning

Part 3 Chapter 4 covers how to divide architecture work across teams and levels.

Why partition?

To avoid duplicate or conflicting work across different architecture teams. Partitions define clear boundaries of responsibility.

Types of partition

  • Federated architectures — teams work within defined scopes
  • Partitions must have clear re-use and compliance relationships
  • A higher-level architecture constrains lower-level partitions

Relationship to iteration

Phase F (Migration Planning) can initiate new more detailed architecture projects — this is how the hierarchy of Strategic → Segment → Capability architectures is created.

Section 3 of 5 · Architecture Patterns

Architecture
Patterns

Part 2 Chapter 4 of the TOGAF Standard introduces architecture patterns as a technique for reusing proven solutions across different architecture engagements.

The core definition

"A pattern is an idea that has been useful in one practical context and will probably be useful in others." (Fowler)

Patterns and building blocks

Building blocks are what you use. Patterns tell you how to use them — when, why, and what trade-offs you make. Patterns combine ABBs and SBBs into proven configurations.

12 / 20

Components of a Pattern

TOGAF defines a standard structure for describing architecture patterns:

Name

A meaningful, memorable identifier — usually one word or a short phrase.

Problem

The intent — the goals and objectives to be reached within this context.

Context

The preconditions under which the pattern applies — the initial state before applying it.

Forces

Relevant constraints and how they interact. The trade-offs that make the problem difficult.

Solution

The architectural resolution — how the forces are resolved.

Resulting context

The state after applying the pattern, including unresolved forces and related patterns.

13 / 20

Patterns & the Architecture Continuum

Architecture patterns sit at different levels of the Enterprise Continuum:

Foundation Architecture patterns

Apply to all enterprises — e.g. the TOGAF Technical Reference Model itself is a foundation-level pattern.

Common Systems patterns

Apply across multiple enterprises in similar contexts — e.g. SOA patterns, Cloud patterns.

Industry patterns

Apply within a sector — e.g. banking reference architectures.

Organisation-Specific patterns

Reusable solutions proven within your own enterprise — stored in the Architecture Repository.

★ Exam tip

Patterns are referenced in Phases A through D as architecture resources. They don't replace building blocks — they describe how to combine them effectively.

Section 4 of 5 · Risk & Security

Risk Management in TOGAF

Part 2 Chapter 9. Risk management is applied throughout the ADM — not just in one phase.

Risk Classification

Risks are classified by impact and likelihood. TOGAF uses a standard risk matrix with categories from low to critical.

4 Risk Treatment Options

  • Avoid — change the plan to eliminate the risk
  • Transfer — shift the risk (e.g. insurance, contracts)
  • Mitigate — reduce probability or impact
  • Accept — acknowledge and document the residual risk

Initial vs Residual Risk

Initial Risk = before mitigations. Residual Risk = what remains after mitigation actions. Phase G (Implementation Governance) monitors residual risks.

15 / 20

Security Architecture in ADM

Security is a cross-cutting concern — it appears in every ADM phase, not just Technology Architecture.

Phase A

Security requirements identified as stakeholder concerns. Security risks noted in Architecture Vision.

Phase B

Business security requirements, roles, responsibilities, compliance obligations.

Phase C

Data classification, application security patterns, access controls.

Phase D

Security infrastructure — firewalls, identity management, encryption, network segmentation.

★ Exam tip

Security is not just Phase D. The TOGAF Standard explicitly states that security requirements must be addressed across all phases. Foundation questions test this cross-phase awareness.

16 / 20

SOA in the ADM

Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA) is handled as an architectural style — the ADM is adapted, not replaced.

Primary home: Phase C

Services are defined as Architecture Building Blocks (ABBs) in Phase C — Application Architecture. Services map to SBBs during implementation.

SOA across phases

  • Phase A: SOA strategic direction established
  • Phase B: Business services identified
  • Phase C: Application services designed as ABBs
  • Phase D: Technology platform for services defined
★ Exam tip

SOA services appear as ABBs in the Application Architecture (Phase C). They become SBBs when specific products are selected in Phase D or E.

Section 5 of 5 · Business Transformation Readiness

Business Transformation
Readiness Assessment

Part 2 Chapter 8. A structured technique for assessing whether an enterprise is ready to execute a transformation programme.

When it's used

Primarily in Phase A (assess readiness) and Phase E (confirm readiness before finalising the roadmap).

The 8 readiness dimensions

  • Vision · Desire, Willingness, and Resolve
  • Need · Business Case · Funding
  • Leadership · Governance · Accountability
  • Ability to Implement Change

Output

Each factor is rated on a maturity scale. Risks and actions are defined for factors below threshold. Feeds into the Implementation Plan in Phase F.

18 / 20

Architecture Alternatives & Trade-offs

Part 2 Chapter 10. A technique for choosing between candidate target architectures when there is no single obvious solution.

The Trade-off Method

  • Define criteria — what matters? (cost, risk, time, quality)
  • Identify alternatives — two or more viable architectures
  • Score each alternative against the criteria
  • Select and document the chosen approach with rationale

When to use it

Used in Phase A when scoping alternatives, and in Phases B–D when evaluating different architecture options. Common in Phase E when comparing implementation strategies.
