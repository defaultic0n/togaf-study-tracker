01

ADM Iteration Patterns

Core principle

The ADM is not a one-time linear process. It is designed to be iterated — both within individual phases and across multiple cycles. TOGAF explicitly supports iterating the ADM to adapt to the scale, complexity, and speed of the enterprise's architecture needs.

The ADM cycle is often depicted as a circle — reflecting the fact that after completing Phase H, the cycle returns to Phase A (or the Preliminary Phase if fundamental framework changes are needed). This cycling is not failure; it is by design, reflecting that enterprise architecture is a continuous activity, not a one-time project.

🔄 Architecture Iteration
Iterating across the full ADM cycle — completing B through H and then returning to A for the next architecture engagement. Each cycle refines and extends the enterprise architecture. This is the most common iteration pattern for large enterprises running an ongoing architecture practice.
🔄 Transition Iteration
Iterating within Phases E and F to plan multiple transition architectures. Rather than planning a single massive transition, the team iterates to define a series of manageable transition steps. Each iteration of E and F plans one transition architecture in detail.
🔄 Capability Iteration
Running multiple simultaneous or sequential ADM cycles at different scopes — one at enterprise level and one at a specific business unit, project, or capability level. Each sub-cycle feeds into the enterprise-level cycle through the Architecture Repository.
ADM iteration and scope

Not every cycle needs to address all four BDAT domains. An iteration might focus only on Business Architecture (Phase B) if the driver is business transformation. Or only on Technology Architecture (Phase D) if the driver is a platform refresh. The ADM guidelines allow scoping of iterations to match the specific need.

⭐ Exam tip

The ADM supports three main iteration patterns: Architecture (full cycle repeating), Transition (within E and F), and Capability (multiple simultaneous cycles at different scopes). Iteration is a deliberate design feature, not a workaround.

02

Security Architecture in the ADM

Security is not a separate activity added after the architecture is designed — TOGAF requires it to be integrated throughout the ADM. Every phase has security considerations.

Security across the ADM

Security architecture runs through every ADM phase. Security requirements are identified in Phase A, business security controls defined in Phase B, data classification and application security in Phase C, technology security services in Phase D, and security compliance checked in Phase G. Security is a cross-cutting concern — not a Phase D afterthought.

Security in Phase A (Vision)

Security stakeholders and their concerns are identified. Security requirements and regulatory constraints (GDPR, PCI-DSS, ISO 27001) are captured as high-level requirements.

Security in Phase B (Business)

Business security policies, access control principles, and governance controls are defined. Who can access what data and under what authority.

Security in Phase C (IS)

Data classification (confidential, restricted, public) applied to data entities. Application security controls — authentication, authorisation, encryption, audit logging — defined for each application component.

Security in Phase D (Technology)

Security services in the Application Platform — identity management, PKI, SIEM, network security — specified. Physical and environmental security for infrastructure locations.

Security in Phase G (Governance)

Security conformance requirements in Architecture Contracts. Security compliance reviews at each milestone. Penetration testing and security assessments as Phase G governance activities.

⭐ Exam tip

Security is a cross-cutting concern addressed in every ADM phase — not something done only in Phase D. The exam may present security as purely a technology concern — that is incorrect. Security spans Business (policies), Information Systems (data classification, app controls), and Technology (platform security services).

03

SOA in the ADM

Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA) is a design paradigm that TOGAF explicitly supports through its guidelines and techniques. SOA aligns naturally with the TOGAF framework's emphasis on reusability and the Enterprise Continuum.

What SOA means in TOGAF

SOA organises applications as a collection of services — discrete, reusable business capabilities exposed through well-defined interfaces. In TOGAF, SOA is applied primarily in Phase C (Application Architecture) but its principles influence all BDAT domains.

Service

A discrete unit of business functionality, accessible through a defined interface, that can be independently deployed, discovered, and consumed by other services or applications.

Service Contract

The formal specification of a service's interface — what it accepts, what it returns, and what quality-of-service guarantees it provides. The application-level equivalent of an Architecture Contract.

Loose Coupling

Services communicate through interfaces without depending on each other's internal implementation. Enables independent change and reduces cascade failures.

Service Reuse

A service built for one business function can be consumed by multiple other functions. Directly supports the Enterprise Continuum's emphasis on reusability across the architecture landscape.

SOA and the Enterprise Continuum

SOA services are excellent candidates for Architecture Building Blocks (ABBs) and Solution Building Blocks (SBBs) in the Enterprise Continuum. A well-designed service that delivers a standard business capability can be reused across multiple architecture projects — reducing cost and improving consistency.

⭐ Exam tip

SOA in TOGAF is primarily addressed in Phase C — Application Architecture. It aligns with the Enterprise Continuum through reusable services as ABBs and SBBs. The exam may test whether you understand which phase SOA primarily applies to — the answer is Phase C, though its principles span the ADM.

04

Business Transformation Readiness Assessment

Even a technically perfect architecture will fail if the organisation is not ready to change. TOGAF's Business Transformation Readiness Assessment provides a structured way to evaluate whether the enterprise has the organisational capacity to undertake and sustain an architecture-driven transformation.

Purpose

The assessment evaluates the organisation's readiness to undergo transformation — identifying readiness factors, assessing current readiness levels, and defining actions needed to achieve sufficient readiness before transformation begins.

Readiness is assessed across multiple dimensions. Each dimension is scored and gaps identified. The resulting Readiness Assessment informs Phase E planning — work packages may include explicit readiness-building activities.

Vision
Is there a clear, compelling, shared vision for the transformation? Do leaders and staff understand it?
Desire and Will
Is there genuine desire and political will to make the transformation happen? Are key leaders committed?
Need
Is there a recognised and urgent business need for transformation? Or are people comfortable with the status quo?
Funding
Is there committed funding for the full transformation? Or is funding uncertain and fragmented by annual budget cycles?
Ability
Does the organisation have the skills, capabilities, and capacity to actually deliver the transformation?
Governance
Are there effective governance structures to manage the transformation — decisions, escalations, and accountability?
Enterprise Capacity
Can the organisation sustain transformation while keeping the lights on? Is there sufficient slack capacity?
HR Readiness
Are staffing, training, and change management plans in place? Will people be ready for new roles and processes?
⭐ Exam tip

The Business Transformation Readiness Assessment is used in Phases A and E. In Phase A it informs the scope of the Architecture Vision. In Phase E it feeds into work package planning — readiness gaps become explicit work packages. Know the readiness factors: Vision, Desire, Need, Funding, Ability, Governance, Enterprise Capacity, HR Readiness.

05

Risk Management in TOGAF

Risk management is woven throughout the ADM — not confined to a single phase. TOGAF provides a structured approach to identifying, classifying, and mitigating risks at both the architecture level and the implementation level.

Risk classification

TOGAF classifies risks across two dimensions: initial risk (before mitigation) and residual risk (after mitigation controls are applied). Both are assessed and documented. Residual risks that remain above acceptable thresholds require escalation to senior leadership.

Risk CategoryDescriptionTypically addressed in
Architecture RiskRisks arising from architectural decisions — technical debt, complexity, scalability gapsPhases B, C, D
Implementation RiskRisks in delivering work packages — schedule, cost, technical feasibilityPhases E, F, G
Business RiskRisks to business operations during transformation — service disruption, capability gapsPhases B, E, F
Governance RiskRisks from inadequate oversight — non-conformance, scope creep, dispensation abusePhase G, H
Risk mitigation approach

For each identified risk, TOGAF recommends four mitigation approaches: Avoid (change the plan to eliminate the risk), Transfer (shift the risk to another party — insurance, contracts), Mitigate (reduce likelihood or impact through controls), or Accept (formally acknowledge and monitor the risk). All accepted residual risks are documented in the Architecture Repository.

Risk and the Governance Log

Risks — particularly those that are accepted or transferred — are recorded in the Governance Log within the Architecture Repository. This creates an audit trail and ensures that risk decisions are visible to the Architecture Board and senior leadership throughout the ADM cycle.

⭐ Exam tip

TOGAF's four risk mitigation options are Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate, Accept. Know that risk is assessed as initial risk (before mitigation) and residual risk (after mitigation). Accepted residual risks are recorded in the Governance Log.
