01

Stakeholder Map & Analysis Technique

Stakeholder defined

A stakeholder is an individual, team, or organisation (or class thereof) with an interest in or concern about the outcome of the architecture. Stakeholders can be internal (business units, IT) or external (regulators, customers, partners).

The Stakeholder Map is a key Phase A output — a structured view of all stakeholders, their interests, and their level of influence. TOGAF recommends plotting stakeholders on a two-by-two matrix using level of interest (how much they care about the architecture outcome) against level of influence (how much power they have to affect it).

High Interest / High Influence

Most critical stakeholders — require deep engagement, regular communication, and active management. Examples: CEO, CIO, key business sponsors. These are "key players" who can make or break the architecture.

High Interest / Low Influence

Care deeply about the outcome but can't directly affect it. Examples: End users, compliance teams. Keep informed and make sure their concerns are captured. If ignored, they become sources of resistance.

Low Interest / High Influence

Can significantly affect the architecture but aren't deeply engaged. Examples: CFO, Procurement. Keep satisfied and engage when decisions touch their domain. A single intervention from this group can reshape the architecture.

Low Interest / Low Influence

Minimal engagement needed. Monitor and inform as needed. Don't waste high-value architecture team time on this quadrant.

⭐ Exam tip

The Stakeholder Map plots stakeholders on Interest vs Influence. High Interest + High Influence = key players requiring deepest engagement. The map is produced in Phase A as part of stakeholder analysis.

02

Architecture Concerns & Viewpoints

The concern → viewpoint chain

In TOGAF, a stakeholder concern is a specific worry or interest about the architecture — performance, security, cost, reliability. Each concern leads to a viewpoint — the perspective from which that concern is addressed. Viewpoints are templates for producing architecture views.

This chain — stakeholder → concern → viewpoint → view — is fundamental to TOGAF's approach to architecture communication. It ensures that every stakeholder gets an architectural view that directly addresses their specific concerns, rather than receiving generic documents that may not speak to their interests.

Concern

A specific interest or worry a stakeholder has about the architecture. Examples: "How will the new system affect our response times?", "Will this comply with GDPR?", "What will this cost to run?" Concerns are documented during Phase A stakeholder analysis.

Viewpoint

A pattern or template defining how to construct a view that addresses a set of concerns. A viewpoint specifies: which architecture elements to include, which relationships to show, which notation to use, and which stakeholders the view is for. IEEE 42010 formalises this concept.

Standard TOGAF Viewpoints

TOGAF provides several standard viewpoints: Organisation viewpoint (who are the actors?), Information viewpoint (what data matters?), Functional viewpoint (what does the system do?), Infrastructure viewpoint (how is it deployed?). Organisations can define custom viewpoints for specific needs.

⭐ Exam tip

The chain is: Stakeholder → Concern → Viewpoint → View. A concern is the worry. A viewpoint is the template for addressing it. A view is the resulting architecture description. This chain runs from Phase A (concerns) through Phases B, C, D (views produced).

03

Architecture Views vs Viewpoints

The key distinction

A Viewpoint is a template — it defines how to construct a view. A View is the actual content — what you see when the viewpoint is applied to a specific architecture. Viewpoints are reusable; views are specific to one architecture.

Think of it this way: a viewpoint is like a camera lens — it defines the perspective. A view is the photograph taken with that lens — the actual captured image of a specific scene. The same lens (viewpoint) can be used to photograph many different scenes (architectures), producing different photographs (views).

Architecture View

A representation of a system from the perspective of a related set of concerns. Produced by applying a viewpoint to the architecture. Examples: a security view, a cost view, a deployment view. Views are what stakeholders actually read and review.

Architecture Viewpoint

A specification of the conventions for constructing and interpreting an architecture view. It documents: the stakeholders it addresses, the concerns it covers, the model kinds used, and the consistency requirements between views.

View Correspondence

The relationships between different architecture views — ensuring they are consistent with each other. For example, the technology view and the application view must be consistent: each application component must have a corresponding technology component to run on.

The 4+1 View Model (context)

TOGAF does not mandate a specific view model, but references the concept of well-known models like Kruchten's 4+1 View Model. Organisations may use any consistent set of viewpoints and views — TOGAF provides standard viewpoints as a starting point.

⭐ Exam tip

Viewpoint = template/specification (reusable, defines how). View = actual content (specific to one architecture, what stakeholders see). Viewpoints are defined generically; views are produced specifically for each architecture engagement.

04

The Communications Plan

Purpose

The Communications Plan is a Phase A output that defines how the architecture team will communicate with each stakeholder group throughout the ADM cycle — what they receive, how frequently, and in what format.

Good architecture work can be undermined by poor communication. The Communications Plan ensures that the right people get the right information at the right time — preventing surprise, building support, and maintaining stakeholder engagement throughout what can be a long architecture programme.

Communication frequency

Different stakeholders need different cadences. C-suite may receive monthly briefings at major milestones. Business unit heads may receive bi-weekly updates during their relevant phases. Technical teams may receive continuous detailed documentation.

Communication format

Executive summaries for senior leaders. Detailed architecture views for technical teams. Impact assessments for affected business areas. Governance reports for the Architecture Board. Format is tailored to the audience.

Communication owner

Each stakeholder communication has a named owner — who is responsible for preparing and delivering it. Typically the architect lead for technical communications; a project manager or business analyst for business stakeholder communications.

Two-way communication

The Communications Plan is not just about pushing information out. It also defines how stakeholder feedback is captured and incorporated — review meetings, sign-off gates, feedback loops, and escalation paths.

⭐ Exam tip

The Communications Plan is produced in Phase A as part of stakeholder management — not as an afterthought later in the ADM. It is a formal deliverable, not an informal list of people to call. Know that it covers frequency, format, owner, and two-way feedback mechanisms.

05

Stakeholder Engagement Across ADM Phases

Stakeholder management begins in Phase A and continues throughout the entire ADM cycle. Different stakeholders are most relevant at different phases.

Phase A
Identify & Map
All stakeholders identified. Concerns documented. Stakeholder Map produced. Communications Plan created. This is the foundation for all subsequent engagement.
Phase B
Business Stakeholders
Business unit leaders, process owners, capability owners engaged in detail. Business architecture views produced and reviewed with them. Sign-off on business gap analysis.
Phase C
Data & App Owners
Data owners, application owners, integration specialists engaged. IS architecture views reviewed. Application portfolio rationalisation decisions require business sign-off.
Phase D
Technology & Security
CTO, infrastructure leads, security team engaged. Technology standards reviewed. Compliance and security stakeholders confirm requirements are addressed.
Phases E/F
Programme Stakeholders
CFO (cost), PMO (delivery), HR (change management) engaged. Roadmap and migration plan reviewed and approved. Transformation readiness assessed.
Phases G/H
Governance & Delivery
Project sponsors, delivery teams, Architecture Board engaged continuously. Compliance reviews involve affected teams. Change management communicated broadly.
⭐ Exam tip

Stakeholder management is continuous throughout the ADM — not just Phase A. Different stakeholders are primary at different phases but all stakeholders receive ongoing communication per the Communications Plan. Stakeholder engagement failures are one of the most common reasons architecture programmes fail.
