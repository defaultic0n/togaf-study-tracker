01

Business Architecture — Definition & Scope

TOGAF definition

Business Architecture describes how an enterprise is structured to achieve its business goals — covering strategy, governance, organisation structure, and key business processes. It is the "B" in BDAT.

Phase B is the first of the three detailed architecture phases (B, C, D). While Phase A produced a high-level vision, Phase B develops a comprehensive view of the business — documenting how it currently works (baseline) and how it needs to work in the future (target).

Objective of Phase B

Develop the Baseline Business Architecture (current state) and the Target Business Architecture (future state) that reflect the Architecture Vision, then perform a Gap Analysis to identify what must change.

Business Strategy

The organisation's direction and goals — what it is trying to achieve and why. Business Architecture must align to and support this strategy.

Governance

The policies, controls, and decision-making structures that govern how the business operates — compliance, accountability, and oversight mechanisms.

Organisation Structure

How the enterprise is divided into business units, departments, teams, and roles. Who reports to whom and who is accountable for what.

Key Business Processes

The end-to-end activities the business performs to deliver value — from customer order to fulfilment, or from strategy to execution. Processes connect capabilities to outcomes.

Phase B in the ADM sequence

Phase B takes its scope directly from the Architecture Vision (Phase A). It cannot begin without the approved Statement of Architecture Work. The business architecture developed here then feeds directly into Phase C — which requires a clear understanding of the business before designing information systems.

⭐ Exam tip

Phase B is the Business Architecture phase — not Information Systems (that's Phase C) and not Technology (Phase D). It focuses on the "B" in BDAT: strategy, governance, organisation, and processes. The exam commonly presents scenarios asking which phase addresses business capabilities — the answer is Phase B.

02

Business Capabilities & Value Streams

Two of the most important concepts introduced in Phase B are Business Capabilities and Value Streams. Both are central to how TOGAF models the business in a way that is useful for architecture.

Business Capabilities

A business capability is the ability of an enterprise to perform a particular kind of work, deliver a specific outcome, or produce a specific result. It describes what the business can do — not how it does it.

Capabilities are:

  • Stable over time — they don't change with process or technology changes
  • Independent of how they are delivered — by people, processes, or systems
  • Used to assess maturity — current vs required capability levels
  • A common language between business and IT

Examples: Customer Management, Order Fulfilment, Risk Assessment, Financial Reporting, Product Development

Value Streams

A value stream is an end-to-end collection of activities that create an overall result for a customer, stakeholder, or end user. Unlike processes, value streams focus on the value delivered rather than the activities performed.

  • Show how capabilities combine to deliver value to customers
  • Cross organisational boundaries — involve multiple departments
  • Help identify where gaps or inefficiencies cause value loss
  • Link business capabilities to business outcomes

Example: Prospect to Customer → Involves: Marketing, Sales, Legal, Finance, and Operations capabilities all contributing to one value stream.

Capabilities vs Value Streams vs Processes

Capabilities = what the business can do. Value Streams = how capabilities combine to deliver value to a customer. Processes = the specific activities performed to execute a capability. All three are used in Phase B, but capabilities and value streams are the highest-level tools.

⭐ Exam tip

Know that capabilities describe what the business does (stable, outcome-focused) while value streams describe how value flows to the customer across multiple capabilities. The exam tests the distinction between these two concepts.

03

Organisation Map & Actors

The Business Architecture must document not just what the enterprise does, but who does it. This means mapping the organisation structure and identifying the key actors — the people, roles, and organisational units that participate in business activities.

Organisation Map

The Organisation Map is a diagram showing the structure of the enterprise — its business units, departments, and their relationships. It provides the organisational context within which business capabilities are performed and value streams operate.

Actor

An individual or thing that initiates or interacts with activities. Can be a person, system, or organisation. Actors perform roles — one actor can perform multiple roles in different contexts.

Role

A specific function or responsibility performed by an actor in a particular context. Roles are more stable than actors — the role "Customer Service Representative" exists even if the person in that role changes.

Business Unit

An identifiable organisational entity that is responsible for specific business capabilities. Business units may be internal divisions, subsidiaries, or partner organisations.

Location

Where business activities take place — physical locations, geographic regions, or virtual/distributed environments. Locations affect how architecture is designed and deployed.

Why actors matter for architecture

Understanding who performs which activities — and where — is essential for designing information systems that support those activities. A CRM system must be accessible to the actors who manage customer relationships. A financial reporting system must serve the Finance department. The actor-role-location model in Phase B directly shapes Phase C and D decisions.

RACI in Business Architecture

Phase B often involves producing a RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) matrix that maps actors and roles to business functions. This clarifies accountability and is used to identify governance gaps — areas where nobody is clearly responsible for a critical function.

⭐ Exam tip

Know the distinction between Actor (the individual or thing) and Role (the function performed in a specific context). One actor can perform multiple roles. This distinction appears in both the content metamodel and Phase B exam questions.

04

Baseline vs Target Business Architecture

The core work of Phase B is developing both the Baseline and Target Business Architectures, then performing a Gap Analysis. This three-step pattern — baseline, target, gap — is the fundamental work pattern used across Phases B, C, and D.

The three-step pattern

Step 1: Develop Baseline Architecture — document the current state of the business. Step 2: Develop Target Architecture — define the future state aligned to the Architecture Vision. Step 3: Perform Gap Analysis — identify what needs to change, what can be retained, and what must be retired.

Gap Category Description Example
Capability Gap A capability needed in the target state that does not exist in the baseline Organisation needs real-time analytics capability but currently has none
Capability Surplus A capability that exists in the baseline but is not needed in the target — candidate for retirement Manual paper-based reporting process to be replaced by automated system
Maturity Gap Capability exists but at insufficient maturity level to meet target requirements Customer management capability exists but is informal and inconsistent
Location Gap Capability exists at some locations but not all locations where it is needed Risk assessment capability present at HQ but not at regional offices
Gap Analysis feeds Phase E

The gaps identified in Phase B (and later in Phases C and D) are consolidated and used in Phase E — Opportunities and Solutions to define the projects and programmes needed to close those gaps. Gap Analysis is therefore one of the most important deliverables of Phases B, C, and D.

Transition Architectures

When the gap between baseline and target is too large to close in a single step, the architecture team defines Transition Architectures — intermediate states between baseline and target. Each transition architecture represents a coherent, stable state the enterprise passes through on the journey to the target. These are formally planned in Phase E and F.

⭐ Exam tip

The baseline-target-gap pattern is used in all three of Phases B, C, and D. Phase E consolidates all gaps into an implementation plan. A common exam question asks: "Where are gaps from Phase B used?" — the answer is Phase E.

05

Catalogues, Matrices & Diagrams

Phase B produces specific architecture artefacts — outputs that document the business architecture. TOGAF organises these artefacts into three types: Catalogues, Matrices, and Diagrams. These types apply across Phases B, C, and D.

Three artefact types

Catalogues are lists of things. Matrices show relationships between things. Diagrams visualise things and their relationships spatially. Together they provide a complete and multi-dimensional picture of the architecture.

The Business Interaction Matrix is particularly important — it maps business units against each other to show which units interact, the nature of those interactions, and the information flows between them. This matrix becomes a key input to Phase C when designing the Application Architecture.

Key Inputs to Phase B
  • Architecture Vision (from Phase A)
  • Statement of Architecture Work
  • Architecture Principles
  • Architecture Repository
  • Business strategy documents
  • Existing Business Architecture (if available)
Key Outputs of Phase B
  • Baseline Business Architecture ★
  • Target Business Architecture ★
  • Gap Analysis results ★
  • Business Architecture artefacts (catalogs, matrices, diagrams)
  • Updated Architecture Repository
  • Architecture Definition Document (Business chapter)
⭐ Exam tip

Remember the three artefact types: Catalogues (lists), Matrices (relationships), Diagrams (spatial views). The exam also tests inputs and outputs — the Architecture Vision from Phase A is the primary input to Phase B, and the Gap Analysis is the primary output that feeds Phase E.
