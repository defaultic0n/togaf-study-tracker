01

What is TOGAF?

Definition

TOGAF — The Open Group Architecture Framework — is a globally recognised framework and methodology for developing, maintaining, and governing enterprise architectures. It is owned and maintained by The Open Group, an international consortium of technology and business organisations.

Think of TOGAF as a common language and structured approach that organisations use when designing large-scale IT and business systems. Without a framework like TOGAF, different teams in the same organisation would design architecture in inconsistent ways, making it hard to reuse work, manage change, or communicate decisions to leadership.

TOGAF was first published in 1995. The most widely used version is TOGAF 9.2 (released 2018), which is what the Foundation exam is based on. TOGAF 10 was released in 2022 but exam content still centres on 9.2 concepts.

Why organisations adopt TOGAF

It provides a repeatable, consistent process to align business goals with IT capabilities, reduce duplication, manage complexity, support digital transformation, and make architecture decisions that are traceable and governable.

TOGAF is not prescriptive — it is a framework, not a rigid standard. Organisations are expected to tailor it to their own context, culture, and maturity level. This is an important exam concept: TOGAF must always be adapted, never applied blindly.

⭐ Exam tip

The exam often tests whether you understand that TOGAF is a framework to be tailored, not a rigid standard to be followed exactly. Remember: The Open Group owns TOGAF; organisations tailor it to their needs.

02

The Four Architecture Domains (BDAT)

TOGAF organises enterprise architecture into four interconnected domains, commonly remembered with the acronym BDAT. Each domain addresses a different layer of the enterprise, and together they form a complete picture of how an organisation works.

🏢
Business

Business strategy, governance, organisation, and key business processes

e.g. org charts, value streams, business capabilities

🗄️
Data

Structure and management of logical and physical data assets

e.g. data models, data entities, data governance

📱
Application

Individual applications, their interactions and relationships to business processes

e.g. application portfolio, interfaces, ERP, CRM

⚙️
Technology

Hardware, software, and network infrastructure that supports applications

e.g. servers, cloud platforms, middleware, networks

These four domains are not designed in isolation. Changes in the Business domain — say, a merger — will trigger changes in Data, Application, and Technology as well. TOGAF's process (the ADM) guides architects to work through all four domains in sequence.

How to remember the order

Think top-down: Business drives everything → the business needs Data → data is managed by Applications → applications run on Technology. The sequence mirrors how strategy flows down to infrastructure.

⭐ Exam tip

Know the full names of all four domains and the acronym BDAT. The exam may present them out of order and ask you to identify which is which. Business and Data are often confused — Business is about processes and capabilities, Data is about information assets.

03

Key Terminology

TOGAF has its own vocabulary. Getting these terms precisely right is essential for the exam — many questions deliberately test the boundaries between similar terms.

Enterprise

Any collection of organisations that share a common set of goals. In TOGAF, "enterprise" is broader than a single company — it can mean a government body, a business unit, a consortium, or even a whole industry sector. The enterprise defines the scope of the architecture work.

Architecture

TOGAF defines architecture in two ways: (1) a formal description of a system (the components, how they relate, and governing principles); or (2) the structure of components themselves. Both definitions are used in the exam — context determines which applies.

Enterprise Architecture (EA)

A coherent whole-of-enterprise description — covering all four domains (BDAT) — that provides a strategic context for IT investments and change. EA enables organisations to respond to change in a consistent, controlled way.

Stakeholder

An individual, team, or organisation that has an interest in, or concern about, the outcome of the architecture work. Stakeholders are not just executives — they include developers, operations staff, regulators, customers, and end users. Managing stakeholder concerns is a core TOGAF activity.

Concern

A specific interest or worry that a stakeholder has about the architecture — such as security, performance, cost, or compliance. A concern is what keeps a stakeholder up at night. Architects must identify and address concerns through architecture views.

View

A representation of the architecture from the perspective of a specific set of stakeholders and their concerns. Different stakeholders get different views of the same architecture (e.g., a CEO sees a high-level business view; a developer sees a technical component view).

Viewpoint

The specification (template) for constructing a view. If a view is a photograph of a building, a viewpoint is the instructions for which camera angle and lens to use. Viewpoints are reusable; views are the actual outputs produced by applying a viewpoint.

Architecture Principles

General rules and guidelines that govern the architecture process and its resulting designs. They represent the values and intentions of the enterprise. Example: "Data is an asset" or "Technology independence." Principles are defined in the Preliminary Phase.

View vs Viewpoint — the key distinction

A viewpoint is a template or pattern. A view is the result of applying that template to a specific architecture. Viewpoints are created once and reused; views are created per architecture project. The exam tests this distinction repeatedly.

⭐ Exam tip

Know the distinction between View and Viewpoint, and between Concern and Stakeholder. Also: the TOGAF definition of "enterprise" is deliberately broad — it is not just a company. Any question asking "what is the enterprise?" should be answered with "any collection of organisations sharing common goals."

04

The ADM Cycle — High-Level Overview

The Architecture Development Method (ADM) is the core of TOGAF. It is a step-by-step method for developing an enterprise architecture. Everything else in TOGAF — the guidelines, techniques, repository, governance — exists to support the ADM.

What the ADM does

The ADM provides a tested, repeatable process for developing architecture. It tells architects what to do, in what order, with what inputs and outputs — ensuring nothing is missed and all four domains are addressed consistently.

The ADM consists of 10 phases arranged in a cycle. The cycle nature is important — organisations don't do it once and stop; they continuously iterate as the business and technology landscape evolves.

Prelim
Preliminary Phase
Set up the architecture capability. Define framework, principles, tools, and how TOGAF will be tailored. The "getting ready" phase.
Phase A
Architecture Vision
Define scope, identify stakeholders, create high-level vision of the target state, and get approval to proceed.
Phase B
Business Architecture
Develop baseline and target Business Architecture. Identify gaps between them.
Phase C
Information Systems Architecture
Develop Data and Application architectures. Two sub-domains addressed together.
Phase D
Technology Architecture
Develop the Technology Architecture that supports Data and Application architectures.
Phase E
Opportunities & Solutions
Identify delivery vehicles (projects, programmes). Plan transition architectures.
Phase F
Migration Planning
Create a detailed migration plan. Prioritise projects. Assess costs, benefits, risks.
Phase G
Implementation Governance
Oversee implementation projects. Ensure conformance with the target architecture.
Phase H
Architecture Change Management
Manage changes to the architecture. Decide if changes need a new ADM cycle.
Req. Mgmt
Requirements Management
A continuous phase at the centre of the cycle. Manages architecture requirements throughout all phases.

An important ADM concept for today: the phases are B, C, D correspond directly to three of the four BDAT domains. The Business domain is Phase B; Data and Application (both Information Systems) are Phase C; Technology is Phase D. Phase A (Vision) sets context for all of them.

⭐ Exam tip

Know the order of ADM phases (Prelim → A → B → C → D → E → F → G → H) and what each one focuses on at a high level. The exam tests phase names, sequence, and — critically — that Requirements Management sits at the centre and is continuous, not a linear step.

05

TOGAF Components Overview

TOGAF is more than just the ADM. It is a collection of interrelated components that work together. Think of the ADM as the engine, and these components as the fuel, navigation system, and chassis around it.

Architecture Development Method (ADM)

The step-by-step process for developing enterprise architecture. The heart of TOGAF. All other components support it.

ADM Guidelines & Techniques

A collection of tools, guidelines, and techniques to support specific ADM tasks — such as stakeholder management, gap analysis, business scenarios, and risk assessment.

Architecture Content Framework

Defines what architecture work produces: deliverables, artifacts, and building blocks. Provides a consistent taxonomy for architecture outputs.

Enterprise Continuum & Tools

A classification system for architecture and solution assets, ranging from generic (Foundation) to organisation-specific. Includes the Architecture Repository where assets are stored.

TOGAF Reference Models

Two built-in reference models: the Technical Reference Model (TRM) for technology infrastructure, and the Integrated Information Infrastructure Reference Model (III-RM) for information flow.

Architecture Capability Framework

Guidance on how to establish and operate an Architecture Capability within an organisation — including roles, responsibilities, skills, and processes needed to run an EA practice.

The big picture

All components work in service of one goal: helping the organisation develop, maintain, and govern its enterprise architecture in a consistent, repeatable, and business-aligned way. The ADM is the process; the other components provide the content, storage, standards, and governance that make that process effective.

⭐ Exam tip

You need to recognise all six components and know roughly what each one does. A common question: "Which TOGAF component provides guidance on how to set up an architecture function within an organisation?" Answer: Architecture Capability Framework.
